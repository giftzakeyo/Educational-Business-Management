⚡ Electric Circuits — Worked Examples & Practice

Grade 12 IEB Physics | Term 3

📌 Key Formulas

Ohm's Law: V = I × R
Emf: ε = I(R + r) = V + Ir
Series: RT = R₁ + R₂ + R₃
Parallel: 1/RT = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂
Power: P = VI = I²R = V²/R
Energy: E = P × t (kWh)

Worked Example 1: Ohm's Law

A 12 V battery is connected across a resistor. An ammeter reads 2.5 A.

1.1 Calculate the resistance of the resistor. (3)

1.2 If the resistor is replaced with a 2 Ω resistor, calculate the new current. (3)

1.3 Explain why the current increases when the resistance decreases. (2)

1.1 R = V/I = 12/2.5 = 4.8 Ω ✓

1.2 I = V/R = 12/2 = 6 A ✓

1.3 Ohm's Law states I ∝ V (at constant temperature). For the same voltage, decreasing resistance allows more current to flow — less opposition to electron flow. ✓

Worked Example 2: Internal Resistance

A battery has an emf of 24 V and an internal resistance of 0.8 Ω. It is connected to a 5.2 Ω external resistor.

2.1 Define emf. (2)

2.2 Calculate the current in the circuit. (3)

2.3 Calculate the terminal voltage of the battery. (3)

2.4 A second 5.2 Ω resistor is connected in parallel with the first. How does the terminal voltage change? Explain. (4)

2.1 The total energy supplied per coulomb of charge by the cell/battery, measured in volts when no current flows (open circuit). ✓

2.2 ε = I(R + r) → 24 = I(5.2 + 0.8) → 24 = 6I → I = 4 A ✓

2.3 V = ε - Ir = 24 - (4 × 0.8) = 24 - 3.2 = 20.8 V ✓

2.4 Rp = (5.2 × 5.2)/(5.2 + 5.2) = 2.6 Ω. ε = I(2.6 + 0.8) → 24 = 3.4I → I = 7.06 A. V = 24 - (7.06 × 0.8) = 18.35 V ✓
The terminal voltage DECREASES because more current is drawn, causing a larger voltage drop across the internal resistance. ✓

Three resistors (6 Ω, 3 Ω, and R) are connected in parallel across an 18 V battery. The total current is 12 A.

3.1 Calculate the current through the 6 Ω resistor. (3)

3.2 Calculate the current through the 3 Ω resistor. (3)

3.3 Determine the value of resistor R. (4)

3.1 I = V/R = 18/6 = 3 A ✓

3.2 I = V/R = 18/3 = 6 A ✓

3.3 IR = 12 - 3 - 6 = 3 A. R = V/I = 18/3 = 6 Ω ✓

Worked Example 4: Power & Energy

A household kettle is rated at 2 200 W and operates at 230 V.

4.1 Calculate the current drawn. (3)

4.2 Calculate the resistance of the heating element. (3)

4.3 Used for 15 minutes. Calculate energy in kWh. (3)

4.4 If electricity costs R2.15/kWh, calculate the cost. (2)

4.1 I = P/V = 2200/230 = 9.57 A ✓

4.2 R = V²/P = 230²/2200 = 52900/2200 = 24.05 Ω ✓

4.3 E = 2.2 kW × (15/60) h = 2.2 × 0.25 = 0.55 kWh ✓

4.4 Cost = 0.55 × R2.15 = R1.18 ✓

📝 Practice Activity 1: Internal Resistance

A cell of emf 1.5 V has an internal resistance of 0.2 Ω. It is connected to a 2.8 Ω resistor.

P1.1 Calculate the current. (3)

P1.2 Calculate the voltmeter reading across the cell terminals. (3)

P1.3 An additional 2.8 Ω resistor is added in parallel. Calculate the new terminal voltage. (5)

P1.4 Compare the terminal voltages and explain. (3)

P1.1 ε = I(R + r) → 1.5 = I(2.8 + 0.2) → I = 1.5/3 = 0.5 A ✓

P1.2 V = IR = 0.5 × 2.8 = 1.4 V ✓

P1.3 Rp = 2.8/2 = 1.4 Ω. ε = I(1.4 + 0.2) → I = 1.5/1.6 = 0.9375 A. V = 1.5 - (0.9375 × 0.2) = 1.31 V ✓

P1.4 Terminal voltage dropped from 1.4 V to 1.31 V. Adding a parallel resistor reduces total external resistance, increasing current, which increases the Ir drop. ✓

📝 Practice Activity 2: Series-Parallel Circuit

A 4 Ω and 6 Ω resistor are in parallel. A 3 Ω resistor is in series with them. Battery = 12 V.

P2.1 Calculate total resistance. (4)

P2.2 Calculate total current. (3)

P2.3 Calculate voltage across the 3 Ω resistor. (3)

P2.4 Calculate current through the 4 Ω resistor. (3)

P2.5 Calculate power dissipated in the 6 Ω resistor. (4)

P2.1 1/Rp = 1/4 + 1/6 = 5/12 → Rp = 2.4 Ω. RT = 3 + 2.4 = 5.4 Ω ✓

P2.2 I = V/R = 12/5.4 = 2.22 A ✓

P2.3 V3Ω = 2.22 × 3 = 6.67 V ✓

P2.4 Vparallel = 12 - 6.67 = 5.33 V. I4Ω = 5.33/4 = 1.33 A ✓

P2.5 I6Ω = 5.33/6 = 0.889 A. P = I²R = (0.889)² × 6 = 4.74 W ✓
