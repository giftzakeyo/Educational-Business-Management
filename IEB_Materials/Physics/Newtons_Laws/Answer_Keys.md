## **Worksheet 1: Inertia in Action - Answer Key**

### Part 1: Understanding Inertia
1. **Seatbelt Safety:** Passengers' bodies want to continue moving forward due to inertia. The seatbelt provides the unbalanced force to stop them.
2. **Tablecloth Trick:** Dishes have inertia - they want to stay at rest. Quick pull applies force to cloth but not enough to overcome dishes' inertia.
3. **Space Station:** In microgravity, there's minimal unbalanced force, so objects at rest stay at rest (First Law).
4. **Soccer Ball:** Friction (unbalanced force) acts against motion, eventually stopping the ball.

### Part 2: Balanced vs. Unbalanced Forces
1. Book on table: Balanced, no motion
2. Car accelerating: Unbalanced, motion changes (speeds up)
3. Hanging picture: Balanced, no motion
4. Ball rolling downhill: Unbalanced, motion changes (speeds up)
5. Plane at constant speed: Balanced, motion continues unchanged

### Part 3: Real-World Applications
*(Answers will vary)*
**Example:** Airbag - Inertia causes body to continue forward during crash; airbag provides cushioning force to slow body gradually.

## **Presentation Slides - Teaching Notes**

### Key Teaching Points:
1. **First Law:** Emphasize "unless acted upon by unbalanced force"
2. **Second Law:** Stress F=ma as quantitative relationship
3. **Third Law:** Clarify "different objects" misconception

### Demonstration Ideas:
- Coin on card (First Law)
- Balloon rocket (Third Law)
- Different mass carts (Second Law)

### Common Student Questions:
- "Why do things stop if First Law says they keep moving?" (Friction!)
- "If forces are equal, why does anything move?" (Different masses → different accelerations)
- "Is gravity a Third Law pair?" (Yes: Earth pulls object, object pulls Earth)

### Assessment Tips:
- Look for conceptual understanding, not just calculations
- Watch for Third Law misconceptions
- Check unit consistency in calculations

## **Common Errors to Watch For**

### Conceptual Errors:
- Confusing mass and weight
- Thinking Third Law forces cancel
- Forgetting friction in real-world problems
- Misapplying which law to which situation

### Calculation Errors:
- Unit inconsistencies (g vs. kg)
- Algebraic mistakes in F=ma
- Sign errors in vector problems
- Rounding too early

### Experimental Errors:
- Not accounting for friction
- Poor timing technique
- Measurement inaccuracies
- Data recording mistakes
