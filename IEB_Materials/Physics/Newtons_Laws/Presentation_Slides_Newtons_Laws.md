## **Slide 4: Newton's First Law - The Law of Inertia**
**Statement:**  
"An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in motion with the same speed and in the same direction unless acted upon by an unbalanced force."

**Key Concepts:**
- **Inertia:** Resistance to change in motion
- **Balanced Forces:** Net force = 0, no change in motion
- **Unbalanced Forces:** Net force ≠ 0, change in motion

**Visual:** Diagram showing balanced vs. unbalanced forces

---

## **Slide 5: First Law Demonstrations**
**Everyday Examples:**
1. **Seatbelts:** Why you need them in cars
2. **Tablecloth Trick:** Dishes stay put
3. **Coin Drop:** Coin falls into glass
4. **Ice Skating:** Glide continues with minimal friction

**Think About It:**  
Why do astronauts float in space?  
*(Answer: No significant unbalanced forces)*

## **Slide 6: Newton's Second Law - F=ma**
**Statement:**  
"The acceleration of an object is directly proportional to the net force acting on it and inversely proportional to its mass."

**Formula:** **F = m × a**
- F = Force (Newtons, N)
- m = Mass (kilograms, kg)
- a = Acceleration (m/s²)

**Remember:** 1 N = 1 kg·m/s²

---

## **Slide 7: Understanding F=ma**
**Direct Proportion:**  
More force → More acceleration  
*(If mass is constant)*

**Inverse Proportion:**  
More mass → Less acceleration  
*(If force is constant)*

**Visual:** Graphs showing F vs. a and m vs. a relationships

**Example Calculation:**  
Car (1000 kg) accelerates at 2 m/s²  
F = m × a = 1000 × 2 = 2000 N

## **Slide 8: Second Law Applications**
**Real-World Examples:**
1. **Car Design:** More powerful engine = faster acceleration
2. **Sports:** Lighter baseball bat = easier to swing fast
3. **Rocketry:** Less mass = more acceleration with same thrust
4. **Shopping:** Full cart harder to push than empty one

**Think About It:**  
Why are sports cars lightweight?  
*(Answer: Less mass means more acceleration with same engine force)*

## **Slide 9: Newton's Third Law - Action-Reaction**
**Statement:**  
"For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction."

**Key Points:**
- Forces always occur in PAIRS
- Action and reaction act on DIFFERENT objects
- Equal in magnitude, opposite in direction

**Common Misconception:**  
"Forces cancel" → WRONG! They act on different objects.

---

## **Slide 10: Third Law Examples**
**Action-Reaction Pairs:**
1. **Walking:** Foot pushes ground back, ground pushes you forward
2. **Rocket:** Exhaust pushes down, rocket pushes up
3. **Swimming:** Hands push water back, water pushes you forward
4. **Ball Bounce:** Ball pushes floor down, floor pushes ball up

**Visual:** Diagrams showing force pairs

---

## **Slide 11: Common Misconceptions**
**Myth 1:** "Bigger object exerts bigger force"  
**Truth:** Forces in a pair are ALWAYS equal

**Myth 2:** "Action-reaction forces cancel"  
**Truth:** They act on different objects, so they don't cancel

**Myth 3:** "Third Law causes all motion"  
**Truth:** It explains HOW forces work in pairs

**Think About It:**  
In a car crash, do both vehicles experience the same force?  
*(Answer: YES - Third Law says forces are equal)*

## **Slide 12: All Three Laws Together**
**Example: Basketball Jump Shot**

1. **First Law:** Player at rest stays at rest until muscles apply force
2. **Second Law:** F=ma determines how high they jump (force from legs, mass of body)
3. **Third Law:** Feet push floor down, floor pushes player up

**Visual:** Sequence showing all three laws in action

## **Slide 14: Problem-Solving Strategy**
**STEP Approach:**
1. **S**ketch the situation
2. **T**hink about which law applies
3. **E**xecute calculations
4. **P**hysically interpret results

**Example Problem:**  
A 5 kg box is pushed with 20 N force on a frictionless surface.  
Find acceleration.  
**Solution:** a = F/m = 20/5 = 4 m/s²

## **Slide 15: Lab Activity Preview**
**Experiment:** Verifying F=ma  
**Materials:** Cart, masses, pulley, timer, meter stick  
**Procedure:**  
1. Vary force (hanging mass), measure acceleration  
2. Vary cart mass, measure acceleration  
3. Graph results, verify F=ma

**Expected Results:**  
- Straight line through origin for F vs. a  
- Inverse relationship for a vs. m

## **Slide 19: Interactive Quiz**
**Question 1:** Why do you need to lean forward when a bus starts moving?  
*(Answer: Inertia - your body wants to stay at rest)*

**Question 2:** If you push a wall with 50 N force, how hard does it push back?  
*(Answer: 50 N - Third Law)*

**Question 3:** A 10 N force accelerates a 2 kg mass at ______ m/s².  
*(Answer: 5 m/s²)*

**Question 4:** True or False: Heavier objects fall faster.  
*(Answer: False - acceleration due to gravity is constant)*
