## Worked Example 1: DC Motor

**1.1** Energy conversion:
Electrical energy → kinetic energy (mechanical energy) ✓

**1.2** Maximum torque orientation:
The coil experiences maximum torque when it is **parallel** to the magnetic field (i.e., the plane of the coil is parallel to the field lines). At this position, the perpendicular distance between the forces is greatest, giving maximum torque. ✓

**1.3** Fleming's Left Hand Rule:
If the thumb, forefinger, and middle finger of the left hand are held mutually perpendicular:
- **Forefinger** → direction of magnetic field (N to S)
- **Middle finger** → direction of current (+ to -)
- **Thumb** → direction of force (motion)
Used for MOTORS (current-carrying conductor in a magnetic field). ✓

**1.4** Purpose of split-ring commutator:
The split-ring commutator **reverses the direction of current** in the coil every half-turn. This ensures that the forces on each side of the coil continue to rotate it in the **same direction** throughout the full rotation. Without it, the coil would oscillate and stop. ✓

## Worked Example 2: AC Generator

**2.1** Type of current:
Alternating current (AC) ✓

**2.2** Peak voltage:
V_max (peak) = 20 V ✓

**2.3** RMS voltage:
```
V_rms = V_max / √2
V_rms = 20 / 1.414
V_rms = 14.14 V ✓
```

**2.4** RMS current:
```
I_rms = V_rms / R
I_rms = 14.14 / 10
I_rms = 1.414 A ✓
```

**2.5** Average power:
```
P_avg = V_rms × I_rms
P_avg = 14.14 × 1.414
P_avg = 20 W ✓
```
OR: P_avg = I_rms² × R = (1.414)² × 10 = 20 W ✓

**2.6** Role of slip rings:
Slip rings provide a continuous electrical connection between the rotating coil and the external circuit. Unlike a split-ring commutator, slip rings do NOT reverse the current — they maintain the same connection throughout, allowing the current direction in the external circuit to alternate. ✓

## Worked Example 3: Fleming's Rules

**3.1** Force on conductor:
```
F = BIL
F = 0.6 × 4 × 0.5
F = 1.2 N ✓
```

**3.2** Fleming's Right Hand Rule:
If the thumb, forefinger, and middle finger of the RIGHT hand are held mutually perpendicular:
- **Forefinger** → magnetic field direction
- **Thumb** → direction of motion of conductor
- **Middle finger** → direction of induced current
Used for **GENERATORS** (conductor moving through a magnetic field inducing current). ✓

**3.3** Difference:
Left Hand Rule is for **motors** (current → motion), Right Hand Rule is for **generators** (motion → induced current). They are opposites — one finds force from current, the other finds induced current from motion. ✓

## Worked Example 4: AC vs DC

**4.1** Advantages of AC:
1. AC voltage can be **stepped up/down** easily using transformers for efficient transmission over long distances ✓
2. AC generators are **simpler and cheaper** to build than DC generators (no commutator needed) ✓
3. AC is **safer** at high voltages for transmission ✓ (any two)

**4.2** Why appliances run on AC:
Most household appliances (heaters, lights, motors) don't care about current direction — they respond to the magnitude of current/power. The rapid changes (50/60 Hz) are too fast to affect operation. ✓

**4.3** Graph:
```
Voltage
   ↑
AC: ~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~ (sinusoidal wave oscillating above and below zero)
DC: —————————————— (straight horizontal line at constant voltage)
```
AC oscillates between +V_max and -V_max. DC remains at constant voltage. ✓

**4.4** RMS calculation:
```
V_rms = V_max / √2
V_rms = 325 / 1.414
V_rms = 229.8 V ≈ 230 V ✓
```
(This is why South African domestic voltage is 230 V AC!)

## Practice Activity 1: DC Motor Operation (Memo)

**P1.1** Diagram (6 marks):
A labelled diagram should show:
- North and South magnetic poles ✓
- Rectangular coil between poles ✓
- Split-ring commutator (two half-rings) connected to coil ends ✓
- Carbon brushes contacting commutator ✓
- Battery/cell connected to brushes ✓
- Current direction arrows on coil ✓

**P1.2** What happens when current is switched on:
Current flows through the coil. The sides of the coil experience forces in opposite directions (Fleming's LHR), creating a turning effect (torque) that causes the coil to rotate. ✓

**P1.3** Why commutator is essential:
As the coil rotates through the vertical position, the split-ring commutator reverses the current direction in the coil. This ensures that the forces on each side are always in the correct direction to produce continuous rotation in the same direction. Without it, the coil would stop at the vertical position. ✓

**P1.4** Two ways to increase speed:
1. Increase the current ✓
2. Increase the magnetic field strength (use stronger magnets) ✓
3. Increase the number of turns on the coil ✓ (any two)

## Practice Activity 2: AC Generator (Memo)

**P2.1** RMS voltage:
```
V_rms = 100 / √2 = 70.71 V ✓
```

**P2.2** RMS current:
```
I_rms = 70.71 / 25 = 2.83 A ✓
```

**P2.3** Maximum instantaneous current:
```
I_max = V_max / R = 100 / 25 = 4 A ✓
```

**P2.4** Two methods to increase output voltage:
1. Increase the number of turns on the coil ✓
2. Rotate the coil faster (increase angular velocity) ✓
3. Use stronger magnets (increase magnetic field) ✓ (any two)

**P2.5** Slip rings vs split-ring commutator:
- **Slip rings** (generators): Continuous rings that maintain constant connection — current in external circuit **alternates** ✓
- **Split-ring commutator** (motors): Split into two halves — **reverses** current every half-turn to maintain same direction of rotation ✓

## Practice Activity 3: Work, Energy & Power (Memo)

**P3.1** Motor lifting mass:
```
W = mgh = 50 × 9.8 × 2 = 980 J ✓
P_output = W/t = 980/4 = 245 W ✓
Efficiency = P_output / P_input
0.75 = 245 / P_input
P_input = 245/0.75 = 326.67 W ✓
```

**P3.2** Motor electrical calculations:
```
P_input = VI = 240 × 8 = 1920 W ✓
Energy = P × t = 1.92 kW × 0.5 h = 0.96 kWh ✓
Cost = 0.96 × R2.15 = R2.06 ✓
```
