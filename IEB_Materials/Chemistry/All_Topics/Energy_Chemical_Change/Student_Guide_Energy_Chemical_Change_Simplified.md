## **Chapter 1: Exothermic vs Endothermic**

### **Simple Definitions:**
- **Exothermic:** Heat EXITS (released) - feels HOT
- **Endothermic:** Heat ENTERS (absorbed) - feels COLD

### **Memory Trick:**
```
EXOthermic = EXit heat (like EXit door)
ENdothermic = ENter heat (like ENtrance)
```

### **Everyday Examples:**

#### **Exothermic (Heat Released):**
- **Burning wood/candles** - gives off heat and light
- **Hand warmers** - get hot when activated
- **Neutralization** - acid + base gets warm
- **Respiration** - your body produces heat

#### **Endothermic (Heat Absorbed):**
- **Photosynthesis** - plants absorb sunlight
- **Cold packs** - get cold when activated
- **Cooking an egg** - needs heat input
- **Melting ice** - absorbs heat from surroundings

### **Energy Profile Diagrams:**
```
Exothermic:                          Endothermic:
   Energy                              Energy
     ^                                    ^
     | Reactants                          | Products
     |   /\                               |   /\
     |  /  \ Eₐ                           |  /  \ Eₐ
     | /    \                             | /    \
     |/______\ Products                   |/______\ Reactants
     +--------> Reaction                  +--------> Reaction
          ΔH negative                          ΔH positive
```

## **Chapter 2: Enthalpy Change (ΔH)**

### **What is ΔH?**
- **Enthalpy change** = Heat change at constant pressure
- **Units:** kJ/mol (kilojoules per mole)
- **Sign convention:**
  - **Negative ΔH** = Exothermic (heat released)
  - **Positive ΔH** = Endothermic (heat absorbed)

### **Standard Enthalpy Changes:**

#### **ΔH°c - Combustion**
- Burning 1 mole of substance in oxygen
- **Always exothermic** (negative ΔH)
- **Example:** Methane: CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O, ΔH = -890 kJ/mol

#### **ΔH°f - Formation**
- Forming 1 mole of compound from elements
- **Elements in standard states** have ΔH°f = 0
- **Example:** CO₂ formation: C + O₂ → CO₂, ΔH = -394 kJ/mol

#### **ΔH°neut - Neutralization**
- Acid + base → salt + water
- **Strong acid + strong base:** ~ -57 kJ/mol
- **Example:** HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O

## **Chapter 3: Calorimetry - Measuring Heat**

### **The Magic Formula: q = mcΔT**
```
q = heat energy (Joules)
m = mass (grams)
c = specific heat capacity (J/g·°C)
ΔT = temperature change (°C)
```

### **Specific Heat Capacity (c):**
- **How much heat** a substance can hold
- **Water:** c = 4.18 J/g·°C (memorize this!)
- **Metals:** Lower c (heat up/cool down quickly)
- **Water has high c** = good for storing heat

### **Step-by-Step Calculation:**

**Example:** 100 g water heated from 20°C to 80°C

1. **Identify values:**
   - m = 100 g
   - c = 4.18 J/g·°C
   - ΔT = 80 - 20 = 60°C

2. **Substitute:** q = 100 × 4.18 × 60

3. **Calculate:** q = 100 × 4.18 = 418
               418 × 60 = 25,080 J

4. **Convert:** 25,080 J = 25.08 kJ

**Answer:** 25.1 kJ of heat absorbed

### **Finding ΔH from Calorimetry:**
1. Calculate q (heat change)
2. Convert to kJ
3. Divide by moles reacted
4. Add sign (+ for endo, - for exo)

## **Chapter 4: Hess's Law - The Energy "Shortcut"**

### **What is Hess's Law?**
- **"The total enthalpy change is independent of the pathway"**
- **Translation:** It doesn't matter how you get there, the energy change is the same

### **Mountain Climbing Analogy:**
```
Base camp to summit:
- Direct route: 2000 kJ
- Via east ridge: 500 + 1500 = 2000 kJ  
- Via west face: 800 + 1200 = 2000 kJ
```
**Total height gain same regardless of route!**

### **How to Use Hess's Law:**

#### **Method 1: Adding Equations**
**Given:**
1) C + O₂ → CO₂, ΔH = -394 kJ
2) CO + ½O₂ → CO₂, ΔH = -283 kJ

**Find:** C + ½O₂ → CO

**Solution:**
- Reverse equation 2: CO₂ → CO + ½O₂, ΔH = +283 kJ
- Add to equation 1:
  C + O₂ → CO₂, ΔH = -394
  CO₂ → CO + ½O₂, ΔH = +283
  ---------------------------------
  C + ½O₂ → CO, ΔH = -111 kJ

#### **Method 2: Energy Cycle Diagram**
```
     C + O₂
       |
  ΔH1  |  ΔH3
       ↓
      CO₂
       ↑
  ΔH2  |
       |
     CO + ½O₂
```
ΔH1 = ΔH2 + ΔH3

## **Chapter 5: Bond Energies**

### **Bond Breaking vs Forming:**
- **Breaking bonds:** NEEDS energy (endothermic, +)
- **Forming bonds:** RELEASES energy (exothermic, -)

### **Bond Energy Values (Memorize Common Ones):**
- **C-H:** 413 kJ/mol
- **C-C:** 347 kJ/mol  
- **C=O:** 805 kJ/mol
- **O-H:** 463 kJ/mol
- **H-H:** 436 kJ/mol

### **Calculating ΔH from Bond Energies:**
```
ΔH = Σ(Bonds Broken) - Σ(Bonds Formed)
```

**Example:** H₂ + Cl₂ → 2HCl

**Bonds broken:**
- H-H: 436 kJ
- Cl-Cl: 243 kJ
- **Total broken:** 679 kJ

**Bonds formed:**
- 2 × H-Cl: 2 × 432 = 864 kJ

**ΔH = 679 - 864 = -185 kJ/mol** (exothermic)

## **Chapter 6: Practical Experiments**

### **Experiment 1: Neutralization Calorimetry**
**What you do:** Mix acid and base, measure temperature change
**What you measure:** ΔT, calculate q, then ΔH
**What it shows:** Heat of neutralization

### **Experiment 2: Combustion Calorimetry**
**What you do:** Burn alcohol under water container
**What you measure:** Temperature rise of water
**What it shows:** Heat of combustion

### **Experiment 3: Dissolving Salts**
**What you do:** Dissolve different salts in water
**What you measure:** Temperature change
**What it shows:** Some exothermic, some endothermic

### **Common Errors in Calorimetry:**
1. **Heat loss** to surroundings
2. **Incomplete combustion**
3. **Inaccurate temperature** readings
4. **Not stirring** properly
5. **Using wrong mass** or specific heat

## **Chapter 7: Energy Diagrams**

### **How to Draw and Interpret:**

#### **Exothermic Reaction:**
```
Energy
  ^
  | Reactants
  |   /\
  |  /  \ Eₐ (activation energy)
  | /    \
  |/______\ Products
  +--------> Reaction progress
       ΔH (negative)
```
- **Products lower** than reactants
- **ΔH negative** (downward arrow)
- **Heat released** to surroundings

#### **Endothermic Reaction:**
```
Energy
  ^
  | Products
  |   /\
  |  /  \ Eₐ
  | /    \
  |/______\ Reactants
  +--------> Reaction progress
       ΔH (positive)
```
- **Products higher** than reactants
- **ΔH positive** (upward arrow)
- **Heat absorbed** from surroundings

### **Activation Energy (Eₐ):**
- **Energy "hill"** to start reaction
- **Catalysts lower Eₐ** = easier to react
- **Higher temperature** = more particles have E ≥ Eₐ

**Example:** Which process has positive ΔH?

**Answer:** C

**Example:** 50.0 g water heated from 25.0°C to 75.0°C. Calculate heat absorbed.
**Solution:** q = 50 × 4.18 × 50 = 10,450 J = 10.45 kJ

### **Question Type 3: Hess's Law**
**Example:** Given three reactions, calculate ΔH for a fourth.
**Strategy:** Rearrange and combine given equations.

### **Question Type 4: Bond Energy Calculation**
**Example:** Calculate ΔH for: CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O
**Strategy:** List bonds broken and formed, use formula.

### **Question Type 5: Explanation**
**Example:** Explain why hand warmers feel hot.
**Answer:** Exothermic reaction releases heat to surroundings.

## **Chapter 9: Study Tips & Tricks**

### **Memory Aids:**
- **EXOthermic = EXit heat** (like leaving a warm building)
- **ENdothermic = ENter heat** (like entering a cold room)
- **ΔH negative** = Exo (think: "negative attitude = pushing heat away")
- **ΔH positive** = Endo (think: "positive attitude = welcoming heat in")

### **Formula Sheet (Memorize These):**
1. **q = mcΔT** (calorimetry)
2. **ΔH = q ÷ n** (enthalpy per mole)
3. **ΔH = Σ(bonds broken) - Σ(bonds formed)**
4. **Specific heat of water:** 4.18 J/g·°C

### **Common Mistakes to Avoid:**
- Forgetting to convert J to kJ
- Using wrong sign for ΔH
- Not dividing by moles for ΔH
- Confusing bond breaking (+, endo) and forming (-, exo)
- Forgetting units in answers

### **Problem-Solving Steps:**
1. **Read carefully** - what's given, what's asked?
2. **Choose formula** - which one fits?
3. **Substitute values** - with correct units
4. **Calculate** - step by step
5. **Check** - sign, units, reasonableness

## **Chapter 11: Quick Reference Guide**

### **Key Formulas:**
1. **Calorimetry:** q = m × c × ΔT
2. **Enthalpy:** ΔH = q ÷ n (n = moles)
3. **Bond energies:** ΔH = Σ(broken) - Σ(formed)
4. **Hess's Law:** ΔH total independent of pathway

### **Key Values:**
- **Specific heat of water:** 4.18 J/g·°C
- **ΔH neutralization (strong acid/base):** ~ -57 kJ/mol
- **Common bond energies:** C-H 413, C-C 347, O-H 463 kJ/mol

### **Sign Conventions:**
- **Exothermic:** ΔH negative, heat released
- **Endothermic:** ΔH positive, heat absorbed
- **Bond breaking:** Positive (needs energy)
- **Bond forming:** Negative (releases energy)

### **Units:**
- **Heat (q):** Joules (J) or kilojoules (kJ)
- **Enthalpy (ΔH):** kJ/mol
- **Specific heat (c):** J/g·°C
- **Temperature change (ΔT):** °C or K
