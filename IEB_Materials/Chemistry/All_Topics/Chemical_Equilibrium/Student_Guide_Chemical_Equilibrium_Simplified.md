# Chemical Equilibrium: Student Guide (Simplified)

## **Everything You Need to Know About Reversible Reactions - Made Simple!**

---

## **Chapter 1: What is Equilibrium?**

### **Simple Definition:**
**Dynamic equilibrium = When forward and reverse reactions happen at the SAME rate**

### **Key Characteristics:**
1. **NOT stopped** - Reactions continue
2. **Rates equal** - Forward rate = Reverse rate
3. **Concentrations constant** - But NOT necessarily equal
4. **Macroscopic properties constant** - Color, pressure, pH don't change

### **The "Busy Restaurant" Analogy:**
```
Customers entering = Customers leaving
↓                       ↓
Forward rate     =    Reverse rate
↓                       ↓
Total customers constant (but people keep moving)
```

### **Everyday Examples:**
- **Soda can:** CO₂(g) ⇌ CO₂(aq) - fizzes when opened
- **Breathing:** O₂ in lungs ⇌ O₂ in blood
- **Cooking:** Water ⇌ Steam (in covered pot)
- **Rusting:** Iron + Oxygen ⇌ Rust (very slow equilibrium)

---

## **Chapter 2: Reversible vs Irreversible Reactions**

### **Irreversible (One-Way Street):**
- **Goes to completion**
- **Reactants → Products only**
- **Example:** Burning paper (can't un-burn it)
- **Arrow:** →

### **Reversible (Two-Way Street):**
- **Doesn't go to completion**
- **Reactants ⇌ Products (both directions)**
- **Example:** Melting ice ⇌ Freezing water
- **Arrow:** ⇌

### **How to Spot Reversible Reactions:**
1. **Physical changes:** Melting/freezing, evaporation/condensation
2. **Some chemical reactions:** With ⇌ arrow in equation
3. **Can reach equilibrium** in closed system

## **Chapter 3: Equilibrium Constant Kc**

### **What is Kc?**
- **"Equilibrium constant"** - tells us position of equilibrium
- **Ratio** of product concentrations to reactant concentrations
- **Constant at given temperature** (only changes if temperature changes)

### **The Formula:**
For: aA + bB ⇌ cC + dD
```
        [C]^c × [D]^d
Kc = -------------------
        [A]^a × [B]^b
```
- **[ ] means concentration** (mol/L)
- **Exponents** come from coefficients

### **What Kc Values Mean:**
- **Kc > 1:** Products favored (equilibrium lies to RIGHT)
- **Kc < 1:** Reactants favored (equilibrium lies to LEFT)
- **Kc = 1:** Neither favored (balanced)

### **Example:**
For: N₂ + 3H₂ ⇌ 2NH₃
```
        [NH₃]^2
Kc = ----------------
        [N₂] × [H₂]^3
```

## **Chapter 4: Le Chatelier's Principle**

### **The Simple Rule:**
**"If you disturb a system at equilibrium, it will shift to reduce the disturbance"**

### **Analogy: Crowded Room**
- **Add more people** (disturbance) → **Some leave** (shift to reduce crowding)
- **Remove people** (disturbance) → **More enter** (shift to increase crowding)

### **Three Types of Disturbances:**

#### **1. Concentration Changes:**
- **Add reactant** → Shift toward PRODUCTS (use up added reactant)
- **Remove product** → Shift toward PRODUCTS (make more product)
- **Add product** → Shift toward REACTANTS (use up added product)
- **Remove reactant** → Shift toward REACTANTS (make more reactant)

#### **2. Pressure Changes (GASES ONLY):**
- **Increase pressure** → Shift to side with FEWER gas molecules
- **Decrease pressure** → Shift to side with MORE gas molecules
- **No change if** equal gas molecules on both sides

#### **3. Temperature Changes:**
- **Increase temperature** → Shift in ENDOTHERMIC direction
- **Decrease temperature** → Shift in EXOTHERMIC direction
- **Need to know if reaction is exo/endo!**

### **Catalysts:**
- **Speed up BOTH forward and reverse equally**
- **NO effect on equilibrium position**
- **Just helps reach equilibrium faster**

## **Chapter 5: ICE Tables - The Problem-Solving Tool**

### **What ICE Stands For:**
- **I** = Initial concentrations
- **C** = Change in concentrations
- **E** = Equilibrium concentrations

### **How to Use ICE Tables:**

#### **Step 1: Write the ICE Table**
For: A ⇌ 2B
```
        [A]        [B]
I      Initial    Initial
C      -x         +2x
E      Initial-x  Initial+2x
```

#### **Step 2: Fill in What You Know**
Example: Start with [A]=1.0 M, [B]=0 M, at equilibrium [B]=0.4 M
```
        [A]        [B]
I      1.0        0
C      -x         +2x
E      1.0-x      0+2x = 0.4
```

#### **Step 3: Solve for x**
2x = 0.4 → x = 0.2

#### **Step 4: Find All Concentrations**
[A] = 1.0 - 0.2 = 0.8 M
[B] = 0.4 M (given)

#### **Step 5: Calculate Kc**
```
        [B]^2     (0.4)^2     0.16
Kc = --------- = --------- = ----- = 0.25
        [A]        0.8        0.8
```

## **Chapter 6: Reaction Quotient Q**

### **What is Q?**
- **"Reaction quotient"** - like Kc but for ANY concentrations (not just equilibrium)
- **Same formula as Kc** but using current concentrations

### **Comparing Q and K:**
- **Q < K:** Too many reactants → Shift RIGHT (toward products)
- **Q > K:** Too many products → Shift LEFT (toward reactants)
- **Q = K:** At equilibrium → No shift

### **Memory Trick:**
```
Q < K → "Queen is Less than King" → Need more products (shift right)
Q > K → "Queen is Greater than King" → Need more reactants (shift left)
```

## **Chapter 7: Industrial Applications**

### **The Haber Process (Making Ammonia):**
**Reaction:** N₂ + 3H₂ ⇌ 2NH₃ (ΔH = -92 kJ/mol)

#### **Optimization:**
1. **High pressure** (200 atm) → Shift right (4 gas → 2 gas molecules)
2. **Moderate temperature** (450°C) → Balance between rate and yield
3. **Iron catalyst** → Faster equilibrium (no position change)
4. **Remove NH₃** → Shift right to make more

#### **Why not higher temperature?**
- Reaction is exothermic
- Higher temperature would shift left (lower yield)
- But need some heat for reasonable rate

### **The Contact Process (Making Sulfuric Acid):**
**Reaction:** 2SO₂ + O₂ ⇌ 2SO₃ (ΔH = -197 kJ/mol)

#### **Optimization:**
1. **Excess O₂** (cheap) → Shift right
2. **Moderate temperature** → Balance
3. **Vanadium catalyst** → Faster
4. **Remove SO₃** → Shift right

## **Chapter 8: Biological Equilibria**

### **Hemoglobin-Oxygen:**
**Hb + 4O₂ ⇌ Hb(O₂)₄**
- **Lungs (high O₂):** Shift right (load oxygen)
- **Tissues (low O₂):** Shift left (unload oxygen)
- **CO poisoning:** CO binds stronger than O₂, shifts left

### **Blood pH Buffer:**
**H₂CO₃ ⇌ H⁺ + HCO₃⁻**
- **Add acid (H⁺):** Shift left (uses up H⁺)
- **Add base (OH⁻):** Shift right (produces H⁺)
- **Maintains blood pH ~7.4**

### **Oxygen in Water:**
**O₂(g) ⇌ O₂(aq)**
- **Warm water:** Less O₂ dissolved (shift left)
- **Cold water:** More O₂ dissolved (shift right)
- **Important for aquatic life**

## **Chapter 9: Common Exam Questions**

### **Question Type 1: Multiple Choice**
**Example:** What happens if you add reactant to a system at equilibrium?
A) Shift toward reactants  
B) Shift toward products  
C) No shift  
D) Catalyst needed  
**Answer:** B

### **Question Type 2: Kc Calculation**
**Example:** For 2HI ⇌ H₂ + I₂, equilibrium: [HI]=0.8 M, [H₂]=0.1 M, [I₂]=0.1 M. Calculate Kc.
**Solution:** Kc = (0.1×0.1)/(0.8)² = 0.01/0.64 = 0.0156

### **Question Type 3: Le Chatelier Prediction**
**Example:** For CaCO₃(s) ⇌ CaO(s) + CO₂(g) (endothermic), predict effect of increasing temperature.
**Answer:** Shift right (endothermic direction)

### **Question Type 4: ICE Table Problem**
**Example:** For A ⇌ 2B, start with [A]=2.0 M, [B]=0. Kc=0.25. Find equilibrium concentrations.
**Solution:** Use ICE table, solve quadratic.

### **Question Type 5: Industrial Application**
**Example:** Explain why the Haber process uses high pressure.
**Answer:** Shift equilibrium toward NH₃ (fewer gas molecules on product side).

## **Chapter 10: Study Tips & Tricks**

### **Memory Aids:**
- **Le Chatelier:** "If you push it, it pushes back"
- **Pressure:** "High pressure likes fewer gas molecules"
- **Temperature:** "Heat likes endothermic direction"
- **Catalyst:** "Speed dating for molecules" (helps meet but doesn't change outcome)

### **Formula Sheet (Memorize These):**
1. **Kc expression:** Products over reactants with exponents
2. **ICE table:** I = Initial, C = Change, E = Equilibrium
3. **Q vs K:** Q < K → right, Q > K → left, Q = K → equilibrium

### **Common Mistakes to Avoid:**
- Thinking equilibrium means stopped
- Forgetting exponents in Kc expression
- Applying pressure changes to solids/liquids
- Thinking catalysts change equilibrium position
- Confusing rate and equilibrium

### **Problem-Solving Steps:**
1. **Identify** if it's equilibrium problem
2. **Write** balanced equation
3. **Choose** method (Kc, ICE, Le Chatelier)
4. **Solve** step by step
5. **Check** if answer makes sense

## **Chapter 12: Quick Reference Guide**

### **Key Formulas:**
1. **Kc expression:** Productsᵖ/Reactantsʳ
2. **ICE table:** I → C → E
3. **Q comparison:** Q < K → right, Q > K → left

### **Le Chatelier Summary:**
- **Add reactant/product:** Shift to use it up
- **Remove reactant/product:** Shift to make more
- **Pressure (gases):** High P → fewer gas molecules
- **Temperature:** High T → endothermic direction
- **Catalyst:** No position change

### **Common Equilibrium Constants:**
- **Water autoionization:** Kw = 1×10⁻¹⁴
- **Weak acids:** Ka (small = weak acid)
- **Weak bases:** Kb (small = weak base)
- **Solubility:** Ksp (small = insoluble)

### **Units:**
- **Concentration:** mol/L (M)
- **Kc:** Unitless (but check exponents cancel)
- **Pressure:** atm or kPa
- **Temperature:** K or °C
