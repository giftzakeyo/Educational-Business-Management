## **Chapter 1: Redox Reactions Basics**

### **Simple Definitions:**

#### **Oxidation:**
- **Loss of electrons**
- **Memory trick:** OIL (Oxidation Is Loss)
- **Oxidation number increases**
- **Example:** Zn ‚Üí Zn¬≤‚Āļ + 2e‚ĀĽ

#### **Reduction:**
- **Gain of electrons**
- **Memory trick:** RIG (Reduction Is Gain)
- **Oxidation number decreases**
- **Example:** Cu¬≤‚Āļ + 2e‚ĀĽ ‚Üí Cu

#### **Redox Reaction:**
- **Both oxidation AND reduction occur**
- **Electrons transferred** from one substance to another
- **Example:** Zn + Cu¬≤‚Āļ ‚Üí Zn¬≤‚Āļ + Cu

### **Oxidizing and Reducing Agents:**

#### **Oxidizing Agent:**
- **Causes oxidation** (gets reduced itself)
- **Accepts electrons**
- **Example:** O‚āā, Cl‚āā, KMnO‚āĄ

#### **Reducing Agent:**
- **Causes reduction** (gets oxidized itself)
- **Donates electrons**
- **Example:** Zn, Na, C

### **Memory Trick:**
```
LEO says GER:
Lose Electrons = Oxidation
Gain Electrons = Reduction

OIL RIG:
Oxidation Is Loss
Reduction Is Gain
```

## **Chapter 2: Galvanic (Voltaic) Cells**

### **What is a Galvanic Cell?**
- **Spontaneous redox reaction** produces electricity
- **Chemical energy ‚Üí Electrical energy**
- **Example:** Batteries, lemon battery

### **Cell Components:**

#### **1. Two Half-Cells:**
- **Each contains** electrode in electrolyte solution
- **Connected by** salt bridge or porous disk

#### **2. Electrodes:**
- **Anode:** Oxidation occurs (negative electrode)
- **Cathode:** Reduction occurs (positive electrode)

#### **3. Salt Bridge:**
- **Completes circuit**
- **Allows ion flow**
- **Maintains charge balance**

### **Electron Flow:**
```
Anode (oxidation) ‚Üí Wire ‚Üí Cathode (reduction)
(negative)           ‚Üď         (positive)
                    e‚ĀĽ flow
```

### **Memory Trick:**
```
AN OX and RED CAT:
ANode = OXidation
REDuction at CAThode
```

## **Chapter 3: Standard Electrode Potentials (E¬į)**

### **What is E¬į?**
- **Measure of reduction tendency**
- **Measured vs** Standard Hydrogen Electrode (SHE)
- **Units:** Volts (V)

### **What E¬į Values Mean:**
- **More positive E¬į** = Better oxidizing agent (easier to reduce)
- **More negative E¬į** = Better reducing agent (easier to oxidize)
- **E¬į = 0 V** for SHE (reference point)

### **Common E¬į Values (Memorize These):**
```
Li‚Āļ/Li: -3.04 V (best reducing agent)
K‚Āļ/K: -2.93 V
Ca¬≤‚Āļ/Ca: -2.87 V
Na‚Āļ/Na: -2.71 V
Mg¬≤‚Āļ/Mg: -2.37 V
Al¬≥‚Āļ/Al: -1.66 V
Zn¬≤‚Āļ/Zn: -0.76 V
Fe¬≤‚Āļ/Fe: -0.44 V
H‚Āļ/H‚āā: 0.00 V (SHE)
Cu¬≤‚Āļ/Cu: +0.34 V
Ag‚Āļ/Ag: +0.80 V
Au¬≥‚Āļ/Au: +1.50 V (best oxidizing agent)
```

### **Calculating Cell Potential (E¬įcell):**
**Formula:** E¬įcell = E¬įcathode - E¬įanode
- **Must be positive** for spontaneous reaction
- **Larger E¬įcell** = more voltage produced

**Example:** Zn|Zn¬≤‚Āļ||Cu¬≤‚Āļ|Cu
E¬įcell = E¬į(Cu¬≤‚Āļ/Cu) - E¬į(Zn¬≤‚Āļ/Zn) = +0.34 - (-0.76) = +1.10 V

## **Chapter 4: Cell Notation**

### **How to Write Cell Notation:**
```
Anode | Anode solution || Cathode solution | Cathode
(oxidation)    (salt bridge)   (reduction)
```

### **Rules:**
1. **Anode on left**, cathode on right
2. **Single vertical line (|)** = phase boundary
3. **Double vertical line (||)** = salt bridge
4. **Concentrations** in brackets if not 1 M
5. **Gases** use Pt electrode

### **Examples:**
1. **Zn-Cu cell:** Zn(s) | Zn¬≤‚Āļ(aq) || Cu¬≤‚Āļ(aq) | Cu(s)
2. **Hydrogen electrode:** Pt(s) | H‚āā(g) | H‚Āļ(aq) || ...
3. **With different concentrations:** Zn(s) | Zn¬≤‚Āļ(0.1 M) || Cu¬≤‚Āļ(0.01 M) | Cu(s)

## **Chapter 5: Nernst Equation**

### **What is the Nernst Equation?**
- **Accounts for concentration effects** on cell potential
- **E = E¬į - (RT/nF)lnQ** (general form)
- **At 25¬įC:** E = E¬į - (0.059/n)logQ

### **Variables:**
- **E** = cell potential at given conditions
- **E¬į** = standard cell potential
- **n** = number of electrons transferred
- **Q** = reaction quotient (like equilibrium but for cell)
- **0.059** = constant at 25¬įC (2.303RT/F)

### **Example Calculation:**
For: Zn|Zn¬≤‚Āļ(0.1 M)||Cu¬≤‚Āļ(0.01 M)|Cu
E¬į = 1.10 V, n = 2
Q = [Zn¬≤‚Āļ]/[Cu¬≤‚Āļ] = 0.1/0.01 = 10
E = 1.10 - (0.059/2)log(10) = 1.10 - (0.0295 √ó 1) = 1.0705 V

### **Concentration Cells:**
- **Same electrodes**, different concentrations
- **E¬į = 0** (same electrodes)
- **Voltage from** concentration difference only
- **Electrons flow** from dilute ‚Üí concentrated

## **Chapter 6: Electrolysis**

### **What is Electrolysis?**
- **Non-spontaneous redox** driven by electricity
- **Electrical energy ‚Üí Chemical energy**
- **Example:** Electroplating, water splitting

### **Electrolysis Setup:**

#### **Components:**
1. **Power supply** (battery/DC source)
2. **Two electrodes** in electrolyte
3. **Complete circuit**

#### **Electrodes:**
- **Anode:** Oxidation (positive electrode)
- **Cathode:** Reduction (negative electrode)

**Note:** Anode/cathode signs REVERSE from galvanic cells!

### **Memory Trick:**
```
Galvanic: AN OX (anode negative, oxidation)
Electrolytic: PANIC (Positive Anode, Negative Is Cathode)
```

### **Examples of Electrolysis:**

#### **1. Electrolysis of Water:**
2H‚āāO(l) ‚Üí 2H‚āā(g) + O‚āā(g)
- **Anode:** 2H‚āāO ‚Üí O‚āā + 4H‚Āļ + 4e‚ĀĽ (oxidation)
- **Cathode:** 4H‚Āļ + 4e‚ĀĽ ‚Üí 2H‚āā (reduction)

#### **2. Electroplating:**
- **Object to plate** = cathode
- **Metal to deposit** = anode or in solution
- **Example:** Copper plating: Cu¬≤‚Āļ + 2e‚ĀĽ ‚Üí Cu (at cathode)

## **Chapter 7: Faraday's Laws**

### **Faraday's First Law:**
- **Mass deposited ‚ąĚ charge passed**
- **Formula:** m = (Q √ó M)/(n √ó F)
- **m** = mass (g)
- **Q** = charge (Coulombs)
- **M** = molar mass (g/mol)
- **n** = number of electrons
- **F** = Faraday constant = 96,500 C/mol

### **Faraday's Second Law:**
- **Same charge deposits masses proportional to equivalent weights**
- **Equivalent weight = M/n**

### **Example Calculation:**
**How much copper deposited when 2A flows for 1 hour?**
Cu¬≤‚Āļ + 2e‚ĀĽ ‚Üí Cu, M = 63.5 g/mol, n = 2
1. **Charge:** Q = I √ó t = 2A √ó 3600s = 7200 C
2. **Moles electrons:** 7200/96500 = 0.0746 mol e‚ĀĽ
3. **Moles Cu:** 0.0746/2 = 0.0373 mol Cu
4. **Mass:** 0.0373 √ó 63.5 = 2.37 g Cu

### **Memory Trick:**
```
m = Q √ó M / (n √ó F)
"Queen Mary Never Forgets"
```

## **Chapter 8: Corrosion**

### **What is Corrosion?**
- **Unwanted oxidation** of metals
- **Most common:** Rusting of iron
- **Electrochemical process**

### **Rusting of Iron:**
**Anode (oxidation):** Fe ‚Üí Fe¬≤‚Āļ + 2e‚ĀĽ
**Cathode (reduction):** O‚āā + 2H‚āāO + 4e‚ĀĽ ‚Üí 4OH‚ĀĽ
**Overall:** 4Fe + 3O‚āā + 6H‚āāO ‚Üí 4Fe(OH)‚āÉ (rust)

### **Factors Accelerating Corrosion:**
1. **Moisture** (electrolyte needed)
2. **Oxygen** (cathode reaction)
3. **Acids** (increase H‚Āļ concentration)
4. **Salts** (better electrolyte)
5. **Scratches** (expose fresh metal)

### **Corrosion Prevention:**

#### **1. Barrier Methods:**
- **Painting, oiling, plating**
- **Prevent contact** with air/moisture

#### **2. Sacrificial Protection:**
- **More reactive metal** attached (Zn, Mg)
- **Sacrificial anode** corrodes instead
- **Example:** Galvanizing (zinc coating on iron)

#### **3. Cathodic Protection:**
- **Apply voltage** to make metal cathode
- **Used for** pipelines, ships

#### **4. Alloying:**
- **Mix with other metals** (stainless steel)
- **More corrosion resistant**

## **Chapter 9: Batteries & Fuel Cells**

### **Primary Batteries (Non-rechargeable):**

#### **Dry Cell (Zn-C):**
- **Anode:** Zn ‚Üí Zn¬≤‚Āļ + 2e‚ĀĽ
- **Cathode:** 2MnO‚āā + 2NH‚āĄ‚Āļ + 2e‚ĀĽ ‚Üí Mn‚āāO‚āÉ + 2NH‚āÉ + H‚āāO
- **Voltage:** ~1.5 V
- **Use:** Flashlights, toys

#### **Alkaline Battery:**
- **Similar but** alkaline electrolyte (KOH)
- **Longer life,** less leakage
- **Voltage:** ~1.5 V

### **Secondary Batteries (Rechargeable):**

#### **Lead-Acid Battery:**
- **Car batteries**
- **Anode:** Pb + SO‚āĄ¬≤‚ĀĽ ‚Üí PbSO‚āĄ + 2e‚ĀĽ
- **Cathode:** PbO‚āā + 4H‚Āļ + SO‚āĄ¬≤‚ĀĽ + 2e‚ĀĽ ‚Üí PbSO‚āĄ + 2H‚āāO
- **Voltage:** 2.0 V per cell, 12 V total

#### **Lithium-ion Battery:**
- **Phones, laptops, EVs**
- **High energy density,** lightweight
- **Anode:** LiC‚āÜ ‚Üí Li‚Āļ + C‚āÜ + e‚ĀĽ
- **Cathode:** Li‚Āļ + CoO‚āā + e‚ĀĽ ‚Üí LiCoO‚āā
- **Voltage:** ~3.7 V

### **Fuel Cells:**
- **Convert chemical ‚Üí electrical continuously**
- **Reactants supplied,** products removed
- **Example:** Hydrogen fuel cell
  - **Anode:** 2H‚āā ‚Üí 4H‚Āļ + 4e‚ĀĽ
  - **Cathode:** O‚āā + 4H‚Āļ + 4e‚ĀĽ ‚Üí 2H‚āāO
  - **Overall:** 2H‚āā + O‚āā ‚Üí 2H‚āāO
- **Advantages:** Clean, efficient
- **Disadvantages:** Expensive, hydrogen storage

## **Chapter 10: Common Exam Questions**

### **Question Type 1: Multiple Choice**
**Example:** In a galvanic cell, oxidation occurs at the:
A) Anode  
B) Cathode  
C) Salt bridge  
D) Voltmeter  
**Answer:** A

### **Question Type 2: Cell Potential Calculation**
**Example:** Calculate E¬įcell for Mg|Mg¬≤‚Āļ||Ag‚Āļ|Ag given E¬į(Mg¬≤‚Āļ/Mg) = -2.37V, E¬į(Ag‚Āļ/Ag) = +0.80V.
**Solution:** E¬įcell = 0.80 - (-2.37) = 3.17V

### **Question Type 3: Electrolysis Calculation**
**Example:** Calculate mass of silver deposited when 3A flows for 2 hours. (Ag‚Āļ + e‚ĀĽ ‚Üí Ag)
**Solution:** Q = 3√ó7200 = 21600C, moles e‚ĀĽ = 21600/96500 = 0.224 mol, moles Ag = 0.224 mol, mass = 0.224√ó108 = 24.2g

### **Question Type 4: Nernst Equation**
**Example:** Calculate E for Zn|Zn¬≤‚Āļ(0.01M)||Cu¬≤‚Āļ(0.1M)|Cu at 25¬įC.
**Solution:** E¬į = 1.10V, n=2, Q=[Zn¬≤‚Āļ]/[Cu¬≤‚Āļ]=0.01/0.1=0.1, E=1.10-(0.059/2)log(0.1)=1.10-(0.0295√ó-1)=1.1295V

### **Question Type 5: Applications**
**Example:** Explain sacrificial protection of iron.
**Answer:** Attach more reactive metal (Zn), Zn oxidizes instead of Fe, protects iron.

## **Chapter 11: Study Tips & Tricks**

### **Memory Aids:**
- **OIL RIG:** Oxidation Is Loss, Reduction Is Gain
- **AN OX and RED CAT:** ANode OXidation, REDuction at CAThode
- **PANIC:** Positive Anode in electrolysis (Negative Is Cathode)
- **High E¬į:** "High and mighty" = good oxidizing agent

### **Formula Sheet (Memorize These):**
1. **E¬įcell = E¬įcathode - E¬įanode**
2. **Nernst (25¬įC):** E = E¬į - (0.059/n)logQ
3. **Faraday:** m = (Q √ó M)/(n √ó F)
4. **Charge:** Q = I √ó t (Coulombs)
5. **F = 96,500 C/mol**

### **Common Mistakes to Avoid:**
- Confusing galvanic vs electrolytic anode/cathode signs
- Forgetting n in Nernst equation
- Not converting time to seconds in Faraday calculations
- Using wrong E¬į values (check table carefully)
- Confusing oxidizing/reducing agents

### **Problem-Solving Steps:**
1. **Identify:** Galvanic or electrolytic? What's asked?
2. **Write half-reactions** and overall reaction
3. **Determine n** (electrons transferred)
4. **Choose formula** based on problem type
5. **Substitute values** with correct units
6. **Calculate** step by step
7. **Check** if answer reasonable
