- **Topic:** Acids and bases

- **Topic:** Acids, bases and salts
- **Content:**
  - Brønsted-Lowry theory

  - Buffer solutions and calculations
  - Acid-base equilibria (Ka, Kb, Kw)
  - Applications in industry and biology

## **Part 5: Key Concepts Simplified**

### **1. Three Acid-Base Theories:**

#### **Arrhenius (Simplest):**
- **Acid:** Produces H⁺ in water
- **Base:** Produces OH⁻ in water
- **Limitation:** Only works in aqueous solutions

#### **Brønsted-Lowry (Most Useful):**
- **Acid:** Proton (H⁺) donor
- **Base:** Proton (H⁺) acceptor
- **Advantage:** Works in any solvent, explains conjugate pairs

#### **Lewis (Advanced):**
- **Acid:** Electron pair acceptor
- **Base:** Electron pair donor
- **Use:** Coordination compounds, not needed for IEB

### **2. pH Scale:**
- **pH = -log₁₀[H⁺]**
- **Range:** 0 (very acidic) to 14 (very basic), 7 (neutral)
- **Each unit change** = 10× change in [H⁺]
- **pOH = -log₁₀[OH⁻]**, pH + pOH = 14 at 25°C

### **3. Strong vs Weak:**

#### **Strong Acids/Bases:**
- **Completely dissociate** in water
- **Examples:** HCl, H₂SO₄, NaOH, KOH
- **pH calculation:** Direct from concentration

#### **Weak Acids/Bases:**
- **Partially dissociate** in water
- **Examples:** CH₃COOH, NH₃, H₂CO₃
- **Need Ka/Kb** for pH calculations

### **4. Acid Dissociation Constant (Ka):**
- **Measure of acid strength**
- **Ka = [H⁺][A⁻]/[HA]** for HA ⇌ H⁺ + A⁻
- **Large Ka** = strong acid, **small Ka** = weak acid
- **pKa = -log₁₀Ka** (like pH)

### **5. Buffer Solutions:**
- **Resist pH change** when acid/base added
- **Contains:** Weak acid + its salt OR weak base + its salt
- **Biological example:** Blood (H₂CO₃/HCO₃⁻)
- **Henderson-Hasselbalch:** pH = pKa + log([salt]/[acid])

## **Part 6: Common Student Misconceptions**

### **Misconception 1:** "All acids are dangerous/corrosive"
- **Correction:** Strength ≠ concentration, weak acids can be safe (vinegar)

### **Misconception 2:** "pH 7 is always neutral"
- **Correction:** pH 7 is neutral only at 25°C, Kw changes with temperature

### **Misconception 3:** "Strong acids have low pH always"
- **Correction:** Dilute strong acid can have pH near 7

### **Misconception 4:** "Indicators change color at pH 7"
- **Correction:** Different indicators change at different pH values

### **Misconception 5:** "Buffers keep pH exactly constant"
- **Correction:** Buffers resist change but don't prevent it completely

## **Part 9: IEB Exam Question Types**

### **Type A: Multiple Choice (Knowledge)**
**Example:** Which is a Brønsted-Lowry base?
A) HCl  
B) NH₃  
C) CH₄  
D) NaCl  
**Answer:** B

### **Type B: pH Calculations (Application)**
**Example:** Calculate pH of 0.01 M HCl.
**Solution:** [H⁺] = 0.01 M, pH = -log(0.01) = 2

### **Type C: Titration Calculations (Application)**
**Example:** 25.0 mL 0.1 M HCl titrated with 0.1 M NaOH. Calculate pH after 20.0 mL NaOH added.
**Solution:** Calculate moles, determine excess, calculate pH.

### **Type D: Buffer Calculations (Analysis)**
**Example:** Buffer contains 0.1 M CH₃COOH and 0.1 M CH₃COONa. Ka = 1.8×10⁻⁵. Calculate pH.
**Solution:** pH = pKa + log([salt]/[acid]) = -log(1.8×10⁻⁵) + log(1) = 4.74

### **Type E: Applications (Evaluation)**
**Example:** Explain why blood pH must be maintained within narrow range.
**Answer:** Enzyme activity, protein structure, oxygen transport all pH-sensitive.

## **Part 12: Sample Lesson Plan (IEB Grade 12)**

### **Lesson: Buffer Calculations**
**Duration:** 1 hour

#### **Learning Objectives:**
- Explain buffer action
- Use Henderson-Hasselbalch equation
- Calculate buffer pH and capacity

#### **Resources:**
- Worksheets with buffer problems
- Calculators
- Whiteboard for worked examples

#### **Lesson Structure:**

**Starter (5 min):**
- Quick review: What is a buffer? (Blood example)
- Why are buffers important?

**Direct Instruction (15 min):**
- Explain buffer components (weak acid + salt)
- Derive Henderson-Hasselbalch equation
- Show example calculation

**Guided Practice (20 min):**
- Work through example on board
- Step-by-step: Identify components, use equation
- Common mistakes highlighted

**Independent Practice (15 min):**
- Worksheet with 3 buffer problems
- Include pH calculation and capacity questions
- Peer checking encouraged

**Plenary (5 min):**
- Key takeaway: Buffers resist pH change
- Exit ticket: "What two components make a buffer?"

#### **Homework:**
- Textbook problems on buffers
- Research biological buffer systems

#### **Assessment:**
- Formative: Worksheet completion
- Summative: Buffer question in next test

## **Conclusion**

Acids and bases provide a crucial link between theoretical chemistry and practical applications in biology, industry, and environment. By connecting mathematical calculations with sensory experiences and real-world applications, students develop both conceptual understanding and practical skills.

The key to successful IEB teaching is balancing theoretical understanding with practical applications, particularly those relevant to South African context and student experiences.

**Remember:** The goal is not just to calculate pH values, but to understand acid-base behavior in natural and industrial systems, and how this knowledge impacts our world.
